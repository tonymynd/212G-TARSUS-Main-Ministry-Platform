Here is a study on patience, drawing from the provided sources:

The Greek word _makrothumeo_ (verb) is rendered as both **longsuffering** and **patience**, and as _endured_. The noun form, _makrothymia_, is also translated as **patience**, endurance[^1][^2], constancy, steadfastness, perseverance, forbearance, and longsuffering[^3]. Both the noun and verb forms of longsuffering have to do with patience[^3] and endurance.

Key points relating to patience:

- **Connection to Longsuffering**: Longsuffering is a type of patience[^1][^3].
- **Fruit of the Spirit**: Longsuffering is a fruit of the Spirit, which one must walk through.[^4][^5]
- **Upgrade**: Longsuffering is also an upgrade to apply while patiently enduring until charity never fails[^6].
- **Qualities**: Patience involves not losing heart and bravely enduring misfortunes, troubles, offenses, and injuries[^3].

The sources also reference another Greek word for patience, _hupomone_[^7]:

- Hebrews 10:36 mentions a need for _hupomone_ (patience) to receive the promise after doing the will of God[^7][^8].
- James 5:10 refers to _makrothumia_ (patience) regarding the afflictions of prophets, then speaks of a different patience (_hupomone_) of Job[^1][^7].
- Hebrews 6:12 & 15 renders longsuffering as patience (_makrothumia_) and endured (_makrothemeo_)[^1].

Patience is also connected to running a race and enduring to the end:

- The "end of the human race" is to be run along "with patience"[^9][^10].
- The pace of the race run by patience is not a grace walk but a grace run to the end of law[^9][^10].
- Hebrews 12:1 discusses running with patience[^4][^11].
- Patience runs the race to the end, which is the end of the law[^10][^12].

The purpose of longsuffering includes allowing all to come to repentance because the Lord is not willing that any perish[^13][^14].

The first level where longsuffering (*makrothymia*) appears is in **Colossians 1:11** and **Colossians 3:12**.  

Then, *ano* (G507) is mentioned in **Colossians 3:1-2**, referring to "things above," where Christ sits—not yet God Himself.  

In **Colossians 3:12**, "Set your affection on things above(*ano*)" reveals that longsuffering (*makrothymia*) is counted among these things above(*ano*), whereas only charity is placed "above"(*epi*). This distinction solves the mystery.  

For deeper *innerstanding*, longsuffering (*makrothymia*) is positioned next to joyfulness (*chara*), yet not at the level of *agaliasis*—the exceeding joy of God Himself. This situates longsuffering on the same "floor" as joyfulness but not yet in the "penthouse" of God.
