Introduction

 

The word “mystery” (‘mysterion’) occurs 27 times & “mysteries” (‘mysterion’) 5 times, for a total of 32 mentions; And interestingly only occurs in the New Testament.

 

Even more interesting is by whom it occurs: by Jesus and by Paul; Notably never by John, similar to how “revelation” (‘apokalupsis’: to uncover and reveal) is never used by John. So then, although “mystery” occurs 4 times in the book called Revelation, which many suppose to be written by John, clues lead to Paul being the writer of The Revelation (uncovering) of St John the divine (aka: uncovering a saint named John thought to be divine). For although John’s writings do reveal some pretty important stuff, he tends to leave out the details, thereby leaving readers confused. So Paul, who writes more NT writings than all 7 other NT writers, notes God is not [author] of confusion, but of peace.

 

So before we begin the topic of “mystery”, let’s notice John’s gospel account uses “Verily, verily”: allegorically akin to “Master, master” = two many masters for a man. For John’s gospel account portrays him as more beloved, but only “according to John”.  It seems why John’s gospel account is sandwiched between Luke & Acts, then followed by 14 grouped epistles of Paul, who clarifies “there is no respect of persons with God”: aka true God’s pure wisdom has no partiality: a constant theme in both OT & NT.
